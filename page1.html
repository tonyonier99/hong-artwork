滾輪放大 ・ 雙擊全螢幕

One cold shower, please.

“I really need a cold shower now,” he said, standing in front of the bathroom.


“What?” The way he linked cold and shower sounded like a completely different phrase in Mandarin—oral sex. I was shocked and had to confirm what he had just said. Only after he repeated himself did I understand, and we both laughed at the misunderstanding.


He is a remote marketing practitioner travelling across Asia, originally from London, seeking a country where he might settle for a while. We met in a queue before a rave. I asked the people directly behind me if they wanted to enter together, as one could gain free entry if three people went in together. “Are you the one with free entry?” he asked jokingly. “If you say so,” I replied without hesitation, smiling subtly.


Inside, I showed him around, and he bought me a drink. The loud music encouraged us to enjoy the atmosphere rather than focus on getting to know each other. Before he left, he asked me to give him a Mandarin name. I wanted it to be meaningful, not just a phonetic translation, so we went to the club’s Chinese ink painting workshop to discuss it. I wrote his name on rice paper as a gift, enjoying the tactile control of the brush I brought even while slightly tipsy. He asked for my contact information and left shortly afterward.


The following week, we travelled to Hualien in eastern Taiwan. Initially, I did not overthink it. After the trip ended, we parted ways. However, he returned to Taipei after a solo trip in southern Taiwan, and this time, we moved in together.


During the three weeks we lived together, we explored Taipei extensively. He pointed out amusing English sign translations and remarked on how gently the Taipei metro sounded. This opened a new perspective for me to rediscover the city after living here for two years. Beyond that, he teased my American English vocabulary. I started to “feel” the subtleties of English rather than merely studying it. His knowledge of London and his playful approach to language made English richer and more beautiful in many ways. I remained deeply drawn to him after he left, and I am glad I recorded his voice beforehand.


This painting represents a conversation that actually happened, combining my fascination with him, our journey, and our amusing misunderstandings—including the memorable moment when a simple request for a cold shower became hilariously risqué.


For this work, I used the reverse side of Wenzhou rice paper. Its surface of fine, velvety fibres conveys my ambivalent emotions, with tap water from the bathroom used as a base. For the vertical and horizontal solid black areas, I employed Wu-Zhu concentrated ink, which contains a high proportion of natural gum, creating a subtle glimmer reminiscent of metallic surfaces to evoke the texture of the tap. For the central bleeding circle, I used Kaimei Kainokuni Sukumi Sumi ink, which preserves line definition in the centre while gently diffusing toward the edges, effectively capturing the subtle vapour and moisture of a bathroom environment.


A seal bearing my Chinese name is placed in the lower-right fifth of the composition, providing visual balance. It is stamped using Guangming seal ink, a vibrant orange-red that remains fully visible even over dark areas of the painting.


The remaining areas of the work express emotion. I incorporated ashes of flowers and plants to reference cigarettes we shared, and during the painting process, I dropped a few tears onto the paper.
